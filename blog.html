How to Calculate Your Freelance Rate in 2026

A complete, no-nonsense guide to pricing your freelance work. Stop guessing, start earning what you deserve.

Why Most Freelancers Undercharge

If you want to know how to calculate your freelance rate, you need to start with an uncomfortable truth: the most common method -- dividing your old salary by 2,080 hours -- is wrong. It ignores taxes, insurance, non-billable time, and the fact that nobody bills 40 hours a week, 52 weeks a year.

Freelancers who price this way typically earn 30-40% less than they should. The gap compounds over years, and many never recover because they anchor their rates to that first low number. The fix is a bottom-up calculation that accounts for your real costs and real working hours.

The Freelance Rate Formula

The correct way to calculate your freelance rate uses four variables: your desired income, your business expenses, your actual billable hours, and a profit margin. Here is the formula:

Hourly Rate = (Desired Income + Total Expenses) / Billable Hours * (1 + Profit Margin)

Each component matters. Skip one and your rate will be too low.

Step 1: Set Your Income Target

Start with what you want to take home annually -- not gross revenue, but the number you would accept as a salary if someone offered you a full-time job doing the same work. For most freelancers in tech, design, and writing, this ranges from $50,000 to $150,000 depending on experience and location.

Do not be modest here. You are building a business, not asking for a favor. If you were worth $85,000 at your last job, start there and adjust upward -- you are now providing more flexibility and specialized expertise.

Step 2: Calculate Your Business Expenses

As a freelancer, you cover costs that employers normally handle. These add up fast:

  • Self-employment taxes: In the US, expect 25-30% of your income. This covers Social Security, Medicare, and federal/state income tax. Outside the US, check your local self-employment tax obligations.
  • Health insurance: $3,000-$12,000+ per year depending on your plan and country. In the US, this is often the single largest freelance expense.
  • Software and tools: Design tools, hosting, project management, accounting software, communication tools. Budget $1,500-$5,000 per year.
  • Professional development: Courses, conferences, books, certifications. Budget $500-$2,000 per year to stay competitive.
  • Other overhead: Coworking space, equipment upgrades, accounting fees, legal fees, business insurance. $1,000-$5,000 per year.

Add every recurring cost. A common mistake is forgetting about quarterly tax payments, retirement contributions, and equipment replacement cycles. When in doubt, overestimate.

Step 3: Determine Your Billable Hours

This is where most freelancers get it wrong. You will not bill 40 hours per week. A realistic breakdown looks like this:

  • Working weeks per year: Start with 52, subtract vacation (2-4 weeks), sick days (1 week), and holidays (1 week). Most freelancers work 46-48 weeks.
  • Hours per week: 35-45 hours is typical. Some weeks are more, some less.
  • Billable percentage: This is the critical number. You spend significant time on sales, proposals, invoicing, email, bookkeeping, marketing, and learning. Most freelancers bill 60-70% of their working hours. New freelancers often bill only 50%.

At 48 weeks, 40 hours per week, and 65% billable time, you get 1,248 billable hours per year. That is 40% fewer hours than the 2,080 that salary-to-hourly converters assume.

Step 4: Add a Profit Margin

Your rate should not just cover costs -- it should generate profit. A 10-20% margin on top of your break-even rate gives you a buffer for slow months, lets you invest in your business, and accounts for the risk you carry as a solo operator.

Think of it this way: if your break-even hourly rate is $70, a 15% margin brings you to $80.50. That extra $10.50 per hour adds up to over $13,000 per year in profit -- money for savings, equipment, or simply surviving a dry spell.

Five Common Pricing Mistakes

  1. Using your old salary as the baseline. Your salary did not include employer-paid taxes, benefits, equipment, or office space. You need to charge more, not the same.
  2. Charging by the hour when you should charge by the project. Hourly rates punish efficiency. Once you know your hourly floor, use it to estimate project costs -- then quote a flat fee with a scope document.
  3. Ignoring non-billable time. If you bill 65% of your time but price as if you bill 100%, you are working for 35% less than you think.
  4. Never raising rates. Review your rates every 6 months. Your skills improve, your costs increase, and inflation erodes your purchasing power. Existing clients can be grandfathered for 3-6 months, but new clients should always get your current rate.
  5. Racing to the bottom. Competing on price attracts budget clients who will demand the most revisions, pay the latest, and negotiate the hardest. Compete on expertise and reliability instead.

When to Use Different Pricing Models

Hourly: Best for ongoing retainers, maintenance work, and situations where scope is genuinely uncertain. Protects you when clients change their minds frequently.

Project-based: Best for well-defined deliverables. Calculate your hourly rate times estimated hours, then add 15-25% for scope creep. You get rewarded for working efficiently.

Value-based: Best for high-impact work where the deliverable generates measurable revenue for the client. If your landing page redesign will generate $50,000 in additional sales, charging $5,000 is reasonable regardless of hours spent.

Regardless of which model you use, knowing your hourly floor is essential. It is the number below which you lose money, no matter how you package the work.
